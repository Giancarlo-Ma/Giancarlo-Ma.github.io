Shakespeare and Dictionaries

We will use dictionaries to approximate the entire works of Shakespeare! We‚Äôre going to use a bigram language model. Here‚Äôs the idea: We start with some word ‚Äì we‚Äôll use ‚ÄúThe‚Äù as an example. Then we look through all of the texts of Shakespeare and for every instance of ‚ÄúThe‚Äù we record the word that follows ‚ÄúThe‚Äù and add it to a list, known as the successors of ‚ÄúThe‚Äù. Now suppose we‚Äôve done this for every word Shakespeare has used, ever.

Let‚Äôs go back to ‚ÄúThe‚Äù. Now, we randomly choose a word from this list, say ‚Äúcat‚Äù. Then we look up the successors of ‚Äúcat‚Äù and randomly choose a word from that list, and we continue this process. This eventually will terminate in a period (‚Äú.‚Äù) and we will have generated a Shakespearean sentence!

The object that we‚Äôll be looking things up in is called a ‚Äúsuccessor table‚Äù, although really it‚Äôs just a dictionary. The keys in this dictionary are words, and the values are lists of successors to those words.
